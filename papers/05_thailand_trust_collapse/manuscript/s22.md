## 2.2 Theoretical Framework: Legitimacy Buffers, Evaluative Transitions, and Preference Cascades

The institutional trust literature has converged on a broad distinction between two modes of citizen evaluation. Easton's [-@easton1975] classic formulation separates diffuse support — generalized attachment to an institution rooted in socialization, identity, and normative legitimacy — from specific support, which tracks performance and is contingent on outcomes. The distinction matters because diffuse support functions as a reservoir: it sustains institutions through periods of poor performance, absorbs shocks that specific support cannot, and decays slowly when it decays at all. Claassen and Magalhães [-@claassen2021] demonstrate this empirically across democracies, showing that satisfaction with democratic performance fluctuates with economic conditions and violent crime, while diffuse support for democracy as a system remains largely impervious to such shocks. Diffuse support is, in their framing, the "deep structure" of democratic legitimacy — the part that doesn't move.

But what happens when it does?

Valgarðsson, Jennings, and Stoker [-@valgarðsson2025] provide the global baseline. Their Bayesian dynamic latent trait analysis of 143 countries between 1958 and 2019 finds that trust in implementing institutions — police, military, judiciary — has been globally stable or rising, even as trust in representative institutions (parliaments, political parties) has generally declined. The divergence matters: it suggests that citizens in most countries maintain diffuse attachment to the coercive and administrative apparatus of the state even when they lose confidence in its political leadership. Thailand's military trust trajectory — from among the highest in Southeast Asia to a 57% "no trust at all" reading in Wave 6 — represents a dramatic deviation from this global pattern. Something specific to Thailand broke the implementing-institution trust floor that holds nearly everywhere else.

The argument developed here identifies three analytically distinct processes that, in sequence, produced this outcome. Each draws on established theoretical resources but their combination — and particularly the temporal structure of their interaction — constitutes the paper's theoretical contribution.

### The Evaluative Mode Transition

The first process is a shift in the *basis* on which citizens evaluated the military, from diffuse to specific support. This is not a decline in trust levels but a change in the cognitive framework generating those levels. The distinction is consequential because diffuse and specific support respond to different inputs and decay at different rates.

Prior to 2001, Thai citizens' trust in the military rested substantially on diffuse foundations. The armed forces occupied a position of institutional reverence reinforced by three pillars: the monarchy's sanctification of military guardianship, Buddhism's cultural embedding of hierarchical deference, and the Cold War-era narrative of the military as national protector [@chambers2013; @connors2003]. Survey evidence is unavailable for this earlier period, but the extraordinarily high trust levels recorded in Wave 1 (2001–2003) — a mean of 3.07 on a 4-point scale, with only 3% reporting "no trust at all" — are consistent with a population evaluating the military through diffuse rather than performance-contingent lenses. Trust at these levels does not reflect careful assessment of institutional outputs. It reflects taken-for-granted legitimacy.

The Thaksin government (2001–2006) initiated, as a byproduct of its populist governance model, a transition in evaluative mode. Thaksin's rural health coverage, village funds, and debt moratoria demonstrated — viscerally, not abstractly — that state institutions could be evaluated on whether they delivered tangible benefits to ordinary citizens [@phongpaichit2004; @hewison2010]. The mechanism is not that Thaksin raised expectations about the military specifically. He restructured how citizens related to *all* institutions, shifting the default evaluative posture from deference to assessment. Dalton's [-@dalton2004] cognitive mobilization framework captures part of this process — rising education and information access equip citizens for critical evaluation — but it describes a gradual, society-wide trend that cannot explain Thailand's timing or institutional specificity. What happened under Thaksin was faster and more targeted: a demonstration effect in which responsive governance made performance-contingent evaluation cognitively available to populations that had previously lacked a reference point for it.

Critically, this evaluative mode shift is asymmetric. Once citizens learn to assess institutions on performance, the prior stance of unconditional deference becomes psychologically difficult to recover. The asymmetry echoes prospect theory's reference dependence [@kahneman1979; @samuelson1988]: the Thaksin era established a reference point for governance quality, and subsequent institutional performance was evaluated as gains or losses relative to that anchor rather than in absolute terms. Citizens who have experienced responsive governance do not simply forget that experience when it is withdrawn. They discount. They compare.

### The Monarchical Buffer

If the evaluative transition had operated alone and without constraint, military trust should have begun declining immediately after the 2006 coup, when the military conspicuously failed to deliver governance comparable to what Thaksin had provided. It did not. Military trust actually *rose* slightly through Wave 4 (2014–2016), a pattern that is inconsistent with any simple performance-evaluation account but entirely consistent with a buffering mechanism.

That buffer was the monarchy. King Bhumibol Adulyadej's personal legitimacy operated as an institutional umbrella that extended diffuse support to the military-monarchy nexus even as specific evaluations of military governance deteriorated [@hewison2010; @chambers2013]. The mechanism was not merely symbolic. Bhumibol's interventions in political crises — most visibly in 1992 — had established the monarchy as the ultimate arbiter of Thai political legitimacy. Military coups were tolerable precisely because the king could be understood as having sanctioned or at least acquiesced to them. The military governed, in a meaningful sense, on borrowed legitimacy.

Bhumibol's death in October 2016 removed this buffer. His successor, Vajiralongkorn (Rama X), neither inherited his father's moral authority nor sought to replicate his role as above-politics arbiter [@pavin2020]. The institution of the monarchy did not disappear, but its capacity to extend diffuse legitimacy to the military evaporated. The military was now exposed — for the first time since the evaluative transition began — to unmediated performance evaluation by a citizenry that had spent fifteen years developing the cognitive tools and reference points for exactly that kind of assessment.

The pre-trend data support this interpretation. Military trust remained stable or rising through Wave 4 (fieldwork completed before Bhumibol's death) and began its steep decline in Wave 5 (2018–2019), the first post-Bhumibol survey wave. The timing is inconsistent with accounts that attribute the decline to any single post-2019 event. The structural preconditions — evaluative transition plus buffer removal — were in place before the protests began.

### The Preference Cascade

The 2020–2021 pro-democracy protests are the third element: the coordination event that transformed what had been a private, atomized reassessment of military legitimacy into a publicly visible, socially reinforced phenomenon. The mechanism here draws directly on Kuran's [-@kuran1991] theory of preference falsification and revolutionary cascades, extended by recent work on endogenous popularity in authoritarian contexts [@buckley2023].

Kuran's framework explains why regimes that appear stable can collapse rapidly. Citizens who privately doubt the regime express public support to avoid social or legal sanction. This preference falsification creates pluralistic ignorance — each doubter believes they are isolated. A coordination event disrupts this equilibrium by revealing that private dissatisfaction is more widespread than anyone realized, which reduces the reputational cost of expressing dissent and triggers a cascade of public preference revelation.

Thailand's 2020 protests fit this template with remarkable precision. The dissolution of the Future Forward Party in February 2020 provided an initial shock, but it was the protests' unprecedented willingness to publicly criticize the monarchy — violating a taboo enforced by lèse-majesté law — that shattered the preference falsification equilibrium. Sombatpoonsiri [-@sombatpoonsiri2023] documents how the protesters' polarizing frames around monarchy reform forced a public reckoning: citizens could no longer maintain ambiguous positions because the framing demanded that people take sides. The cascade was not gradual. The Wave 5 to Wave 6 military trust drop (2.23 to 1.62) exceeds the total decline across the previous four waves combined.

Buckley, Marquardt, and Reuter [-@buckley2023] demonstrate, in the Russian context, that perceptions of regime popularity can produce *genuine* attitudinal shifts — not merely changes in expressed preferences — by altering citizens' informational environment about the social acceptability of dissent. Applied to Thailand, this implies that the protests did not just permit people to say what they already thought. The public revelation of widespread dissatisfaction likely shifted some previously ambivalent citizens toward genuinely lower trust, as the informational signal ("most people distrust the military") updated their own evaluations.

### Observable Implications

This three-stage framework — evaluative transition, buffer removal, preference cascade — generates predictions that the paper tests against six waves of Asian Barometer data in three countries.

The first prediction is **institutional differentiation**. If the collapse reflects the military's specific vulnerability as a diffuse-legitimacy-dependent institution now exposed to specific evaluation, then military trust should decline more steeply than government trust. The military depended more heavily on monarchical legitimation and had less performance track record to fall back on. Government trust, by contrast, has always contained a larger specific-support component — citizens routinely evaluate governments on economic management — and should therefore show a less dramatic shift. The prediction is: $\beta_{\text{Military} \times \text{Wave}} < \beta_{\text{Government} \times \text{Wave}}$ in Thailand specifically (H1).

The second prediction is **discrete shock rather than gradual erosion**. If the cascade mechanism is operative, the decline should be concentrated in the Wave 5–Wave 6 transition (when protests occurred) rather than distributed incrementally across all waves. A pre-trend analysis should show no significant downward trajectory prior to the buffer removal (H2).

The third prediction is **comparative divergence**. The Philippines lacked the evaluative mode transition (Duterte governed within, not against, the coercive apparatus's institutional identity) and lacked the buffer-removal event. Taiwan completed its evaluative transition decades earlier and successfully depoliticized the military. Neither should show the cliff pattern observable in Thailand (H3). Taiwan provides the baseline of successful military trust stabilization through depoliticization; the Philippines provides the contrast of maintained diffuse support under a populist who aligned with rather than against coercive institutions.

The fourth prediction is **geographic concentration**. If the evaluative transition was initiated by Thaksin-era governance and catalyzed by the 2020 protests, the steepest declines should concentrate in Bangkok (the protest epicenter) and the Northeast (Thaksin's political heartland and the region most directly affected by his policy interventions). The conservative South, with weaker exposure to both the Thaksin governance demonstration and the protest coordination event, should show smaller declines (H4).

A fifth prediction follows from the preference cascade mechanism: **demographic diffusion**. The protests were youth-led and initially concentrated among politically engaged citizens. If these groups served as early movers in the cascade — the first to publicly reveal updated preferences — then the trust decline should appear earliest and most steeply among younger, more politically engaged respondents, with older and less engaged populations converging toward similar levels as the cascade broadened. The convergence between democratic believers and skeptics on low military trust, observable in the Wave 6 data, is consistent with this diffusion pattern — though it also needs to be distinguished from a mechanical floor effect, a test the paper addresses in its robustness analysis (H5).
