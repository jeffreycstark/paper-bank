\section{2. Research Hypotheses}\label{research-hypotheses}

Based on the descriptive findings, we test the following hypotheses:

\subsection{H1: The COVID Infection
Paradox}\label{h1-the-covid-infection-paradox}

\textbf{H1a}: Personal COVID infection does NOT negatively predict
government approval in Vietnam (paradox)

\textbf{H1b}: Personal COVID infection DOES negatively predict
government approval in Cambodia/Thailand (normal pattern)

\textbf{H1c}: The relationship between COVID infection and approval
differs significantly by country (interaction)

\subsection{4. H2: Trust as Mediator}\label{h2-trust-as-mediator}

\textbf{H2a}: Trust in government COVID information positively predicts
government approval (direct effect)

\textbf{H2b}: Trust mediates the relationship between COVID infection
and government approval

\textbf{H2c}: The mediating effect of trust differs by country

\subsection{5. H3: Democracy and
Authoritarianism}\label{h3-democracy-and-authoritarianism}

\textbf{H3a}: Democracy satisfaction positively predicts government
approval

\textbf{H3b}: Authoritarianism acceptance positively predicts government
approval

\textbf{H3c}: These relationships differ by country (Vietnam shows
dissociation)

\subsection{H4: Economic Impact
(Control/Robustness)}\label{h4-economic-impact-controlrobustness}

\textbf{H4a}: COVID economic impact negatively predicts government
approval (expected baseline)

\textbf{H4b}: Economic impact has weaker effects than trust in
information environment
