## 1. REVISED ABSTRACT

Between 2008 and 2021, Cambodian citizens underwent one of the most dramatic transformations in political orientation recorded in contemporary survey research. Drawing on four waves of Asian Barometer Survey data (Waves 2, 3, 4, and 6; N ≈ 1,000–1,242 per wave), supplemented by the 2017 Cambodia Socio-Economic Survey (N = 3,363), this article traces the arc of that transformation as a sequential process: from post-conflict acquiescence, through a remarkable peak of democratic aspiration and civic mobilization, to a final condition of political withdrawal and regime normalization following the elimination of organized opposition. The Cambodian case offers an unusually clean empirical window into how dominant-party authoritarian regimes produce political quiescence — not through the cultivation of enthusiastic support, but through a process the article terms *demobilization by subtraction*, in which the removal of credible democratic alternatives restructures citizen orientations across multiple attitudinal and behavioral dimensions simultaneously. The article documents a theoretically consequential divergence: abstract democratic commitment — the belief that democracy is the best form of government — remained largely intact across the study period, while democratic prioritization, feasibility expectations, and independent civic engagement collapsed in parallel. Citizens did not abandon democratic ideals; they ceased to believe those ideals were achievable and ceased to prioritize them against competing goods. The findings challenge accounts that treat authoritarian legitimacy as a product of performance or ideology, suggesting instead that the most effective mechanism of regime stabilization may be the systematic closure of the political imagination.

## 2. REVISED DATA, MEASURES, AND COMPARABILITY — New Subsection on Democratic Orientations

[This replaces the existing "Democratic expectations" subsection in Section 1.1. The paragraphs on other domains — political participation, authoritarian governance preferences, corruption, media engagement — remain as written.]

### Democratic Orientations

The current analysis expands the treatment of democratic attitudes beyond the 0–10 expectation items employed in earlier drafts, incorporating measures of democratic commitment, democratic prioritization, and democratic satisfaction alongside democratic expectations. The expanded battery addresses a measurement concern that has become increasingly salient in the comparative democratic attitudes literature: that survey-measured "democratic support" is not a unitary construct but a composite of analytically separable dimensions that may respond differently to political conditions (Bratton et al. 2004; Mattes and Bratton 2007).

*Democratic commitment.* Two items measure abstract endorsement of democracy as a regime form. The first, fielded in all four waves, presents respondents with a three-option forced choice: "Democracy is always preferable to any other kind of government," "Under some circumstances, an authoritarian government can be preferable," or "For people like me, it doesn't matter whether we have a democratic or non-democratic government." The harmonized coding runs 1 (democracy always preferable) to 3 (doesn't matter), such that lower means indicate stronger democratic commitment. The second, fielded in Waves 3 through 6, asks respondents to evaluate the statement "Democracy may have its problems, but it is still the best form of government" on a standard 1–4 agree/disagree scale (higher = more pro-democratic). Both items employ identical wording across all waves in which they appear. Nonresponse rates on these items are low: under 2 percent in Waves 3 and 4, rising to 10–13 percent in Wave 6 — elevated, but far below the levels observed on the evaluative-projective items discussed below.

*Democratic prioritization.* One item measures the trade-off between political freedom and economic equality, asking respondents to choose between "reducing economic inequality" and "protecting political freedom" on a five-point scale (1 = inequality reduction definitely more important, 5 = political freedom definitely more important), with a midpoint option of "both equally important." The item was fielded in Waves 3, 4, and 6 with identical wording. Nonresponse is minimal (1–8 percent across waves). A second trade-off item, pitting democracy against economic development on a five-point scale, is available across all four waves but proves empirically less dynamic in the Cambodian case; it is reported in the appendix for completeness.

*Democratic satisfaction.* Respondents in all four waves were asked: "On the whole, how satisfied or dissatisfied are you with the way democracy works in [Cambodia]?" on a four-point scale (1 = not at all satisfied, 4 = very satisfied). Wording is identical across waves. Nonresponse is low (0.3–6.4 percent). This item occupies an intermediate position between normative democratic support and regime performance evaluation; its trajectory proves substantively central to the normalization argument developed below.

*Democratic expectations.* Three items fielded in Waves 3, 4, and 6 measure perceived democratic quality on 0–10 scales: democratic past (how democratic was the country ten years ago?), democratic present (how democratic is the country today?), and democratic future (how democratic will the country be in ten years?). These items carry the highest nonresponse rates in the democratic battery — reaching 63 percent on the democratic future item and 43 percent on the democratic present item in Wave 6. The elevated missingness, concentrated in Wave 6, is itself analytically informative and is addressed at length in the alternative mechanisms section. Because the expectations items provide the most vivid illustration of the temporal arc documented in this article but rest on the weakest data foundations, the analytic strategy treats them as supporting evidence for patterns that are established more robustly on the commitment, prioritization, and satisfaction measures.

## 3. NEW SUBSECTION: Supplementary Data — The 2017 Cambodia Socio-Economic Survey

[Insert in Section 1.1 after the ABS methodology discussion.]

The ABS data are supplemented by a single cross-section from the Cambodia Socio-Economic Survey (SES), a nationally representative household survey administered by the National Institute of Statistics. The SES wave employed here (deposited with the World Bank Microdata Library as KHM_2018_SAIE_v01; N = 3,363) was collected through face-to-face interviews with an inferred fieldwork period of early-to-mid 2017, based on internal temporal references: the questionnaire references the 2016–17 school year as current, the 2016 commune budget as the most recently completed fiscal cycle, and "additional money paid to officials in 2016" as a retrospective question about the immediately preceding year. This timing places the SES fieldwork in the final months before the intensification of political closure — after the collapse of the Culture of Dialogue but before Kem Sokha's arrest in September 2017 and the CNRP dissolution in November 2017.

Six governance items from the SES are relevant to the present analysis: perceived responsiveness of commune councillors and village chiefs (binary agree/disagree), perceived freedom of speech and freedom of association at the commune level (binary), perceived freedom to vote for any party without fear (binary), and reported payment of "tea money" — the Cambodian vernacular for petty bribery — to government officials in 2016 (binary). These items are not directly comparable to the ABS instruments in scale or sampling design, and are not treated as equivalent measures. Their value is triangulative: they establish the state of perceived civic freedom and corruption exposure in the immediate pre-dissolution period, a window the ABS does not cover. The SES data are reported in Appendix D.

Detailed SES governance modules were not publicly available for the 2018–2020 fieldwork period; a phone-based COVID-focused survey conducted in 2020 carried no political orientation or corruption items. The absence of detailed governance survey data during the years immediately following the CNRP dissolution is consistent with the broader pattern of political closure documented in this article.

## 4. REVISED WAVE 2 (2008) SECTION — Additions Only

[Insert after the existing paragraph on authoritarian attitude measures, before "What is most notable about the 2008 data..."]

The democratic orientation measures from 2008 establish a baseline of modest, largely passive democratic sentiment. On the forced-choice democracy preference item, 61.2 percent of respondents selected "democracy is always preferable," while 30.5 percent — nearly a third — chose "for people like me, it doesn't matter." Only 8.3 percent endorsed the view that authoritarian government could sometimes be preferable. The mean (1.69 on the 1–3 scale, where 1 represents the strongest democratic commitment) reflects a population that leaned democratic but without intensity: a plurality endorsed democracy as an abstract principle, but the large "doesn't matter" share signals the political indifference of a citizenry that had experienced little meaningful democratic choice. Democracy satisfaction stood at 2.97 on a four-point scale — essentially at the midpoint between "not very satisfied" and "fairly satisfied." The democracy-versus-economic-development trade-off item produced a mean of 2.43 on a five-point scale, leaning slightly toward economic development. These are not the numbers of a population invested in democratic politics. They are the numbers of a population for whom democracy was neither an aspiration nor a grievance — simply not a salient dimension of political life.

## 5. REVISED WAVE 3 (2012) SECTION — Additions

[Insert after the existing paragraph on democratic future expectations (9.58), before "The paradox, of course..."]

The broader democratic orientation battery from Wave 3 reveals a more textured picture than the expectations items alone suggest. On the forced-choice democracy preference, 56.9 percent chose "democracy is always preferable" — actually *lower* than in 2008 (61.2 percent), with 30.2 percent selecting "doesn't matter" and 12.9 percent endorsing conditional authoritarianism. The 2012 moment was not, in other words, a peak of abstract democratic commitment; it was a peak of democratic *expectation* and *prioritization*. The distinction matters. Respondents who rated their country's democratic future at 9.58 on a ten-point scale were not necessarily more committed to democracy as an ideal than their 2008 counterparts — they were more convinced that democracy was arriving. The commitment-expectation gap in 2012 — modest abstract endorsement alongside extraordinary feasibility optimism — is the signature of a population whose democratic orientation was driven by perceived possibility rather than by normative conviction.

Where prioritization is concerned, the 2012 data register the high-water mark. On the democracy-versus-equality trade-off, the pro-political-freedom side accounted for 47.1 percent of respondents (25.1 percent "somewhat," 22.0 percent "definitely"), while the pro-equality side stood at 50.4 percent, with a negligible 2.5 percent selecting "both equally important." The population was almost evenly divided, but nearly half were willing to prioritize political freedom over material equality — a level of democratic prioritization that would not be observed again. The "democracy is the best form of government" item debuted in this wave at 3.26 on a four-point scale (between "agree" and "strongly agree"), the highest value it would record across the three waves in which it was fielded. Democracy satisfaction held steady at 2.98, essentially unchanged from 2008.

## 6. REVISED WAVE 4 (2015) SECTION — New Material on Democratic Orientations

[Replace the current paragraph beginning "The democratic expectation measures, however, had already begun their descent" with the following expanded treatment.]

The democratic orientation measures from Wave 4 reveal the first signs of the decoupling that would define the Cambodian trajectory: the simultaneous intensification of abstract democratic commitment and erosion of democratic expectation. On the forced-choice democracy preference, 74.9 percent of respondents selected "democracy is always preferable" — the highest proportion across all four waves, up sharply from 56.9 percent in 2012. The "doesn't matter" category collapsed from 30.2 to 18.9 percent. Citizens who had been indifferent to the democracy question in earlier waves were now choosing sides, and choosing democracy. The shift is consistent with a political environment in which democracy had become contested and salient — the 2013 election, the mass protests, the crackdown — rather than the background condition it had been in 2008. Commitment to democracy as an ideal intensified precisely because the ideal was under visible threat. "Democracy may have its problems but it is still the best form of government" dipped modestly from 3.26 to 3.15 (p < 0.001), a statistically significant but substantively small decline that left the mean firmly in "agree" territory.

At the same moment, democratic prioritization began its descent. The democracy-versus-equality trade-off shifted significantly toward the equality pole: the pro-political-freedom side contracted from 47.1 to 34.6 percent, while the "both equally" midpoint expanded from 2.5 to 8.2 percent (mean: 2.91 → 2.58, p < 0.001). Citizens were declaring their commitment to democracy in the abstract while becoming less willing to prioritize it when placed in competition with economic equality. The pattern is not contradictory; it reflects a population that had learned from the events of 2013–2014 that democratic aspiration carried real costs — political instability, economic disruption, state violence — and was beginning to weigh those costs against the ideal even while affirming the ideal itself.

The democratic expectation measures had already begun their descent. The democratic future rating fell from 9.58 to 7.72 — a drop of nearly two full points on the ten-point scale (p < 0.001). The democratic present declined from 5.85 to 5.06. Only the democratic past held relatively steady at 3.87. Democracy satisfaction fell to 2.71 — the lowest value in the four-wave series — consistent with a widening gap between democratic expectations (still elevated at 7.72) and perceived democratic reality (5.06). The gap between what citizens hoped for and what they observed produced dissatisfaction; the narrowing of that gap by Wave 6, accomplished not by improving reality but by lowering expectations, would produce the opposite.

The configuration of the 2015 democratic orientation data is, in retrospect, the clearest signal of the normalization process in its early stages. Ideals intensified. Prioritization weakened. Expectations fell. Satisfaction declined. The four dimensions, which might be expected to move in concert if democratic attitudes were a unitary construct, instead moved in characteristically different directions — a pattern that the elimination of the CNRP two years later would amplify to its full expression.

## 7. REVISED WAVE 6 (2021) SECTION — New Material on Democratic Orientations

[Replace the current passage on democratic expectations (beginning with the corruption data and ending before "The voter turnout paradox") with the following expanded treatment. The corruption, voter turnout, and international orientation passages that currently follow remain as written, though democracy satisfaction should be removed from wherever it appears in the placebo discussion and referenced here instead.]

### Democratic Orientations in 2021

The democratic orientation data from Wave 6 complete the divergence pattern that first appeared in 2015, now with the full force of the post-dissolution political closure behind it.

Abstract democratic commitment, remarkably, survived the dissolution largely intact. "Democracy may have its problems but it is still the best form of government" registered a mean of 3.20 in Wave 6 — between "agree" and "strongly agree," statistically indistinguishable from its Wave 4 value (3.15; p = 0.080) and only marginally below the Wave 3 peak of 3.26 (p = 0.014). The stability of this item across a period in which every other democratic indicator declined sharply is among the most important findings in the dataset. Cambodians in 2021 continued to endorse democracy as an ideal at rates essentially unchanged from the peak mobilization of 2012. Whatever the dissolution accomplished, it did not produce ideological conversion.

The forced-choice democracy preference item tells a subtler story. The proportion selecting "democracy is always preferable" declined from its Wave 4 peak of 74.9 percent to 66.5 percent — still a clear majority, but a significant contraction. The critical diagnostic is *where the movement went*. The "doesn't matter" category, which had contracted sharply between 2012 and 2015 (30.2 to 18.9 percent), continued to decline to 17.5 percent — its lowest level across all four waves. The growth was concentrated entirely in "under some circumstances, an authoritarian government can be preferable," which more than doubled from 6.1 to 16.0 percent. This distribution is inconsistent with generalized political apathy, which would inflate the "doesn't matter" category, and inconsistent with fear-driven acquiescence, which would push respondents toward whichever option appeared safest. It is consistent with a specific form of pragmatic recalculation: a subset of citizens who had committed to democracy as an ideal in 2015, when the opposition was still present, reassessed that commitment in light of a political reality in which democratic alternatives no longer existed. They did not become indifferent; they became conditionally authoritarian. The distinction between apathy and conditional acceptance is central to the normalization framework proposed here.

Democratic prioritization reached its lowest recorded level. The democracy-versus-equality trade-off declined to a mean of 2.31 (p < 0.001 for both the W3→W6 and W4→W6 comparisons). The pro-political-freedom side of the distribution, which had accounted for 47.1 percent of respondents in 2012, contracted to 21.7 percent — a collapse of more than half. The movement was not uniform across the distribution. The "somewhat pro-freedom" category suffered the steepest proportional decline (25.1 to 8.4 percent), while "definitely pro-freedom" eroded more modestly (22.0 to 13.3 percent). The citizens most susceptible to deprioritization were those whose commitment to political freedom had been moderate and conditional — precisely the pragmatic middle that the normalization framework predicts would be most responsive to the closure of democratic possibility. Meanwhile, the "both equally important" midpoint expanded from 2.5 percent in 2012 to 15.1 percent — a sixfold increase in the population that had retreated to noncommittal ambivalence. These respondents are the attitudinal analogue of the behavioral withdrawal documented in the participation data: citizens who previously held a directional political preference and have ceased to hold one.

The democratic expectation items continued their descent, though the estimates must be interpreted with caution given the elevated nonresponse rates discussed below. The democratic future rating fell from 7.72 in 2015 to 6.67 in 2021 (p < 0.001), extending the decline from the extraordinary 9.58 recorded in 2012 to a level that, while still modestly optimistic, represents a drop of nearly three points — roughly 30 percent of the available scale range — across nine years. Democratic present stood at 5.77, essentially unchanged from 2012 (5.85), while the democratic past rose to 4.78 — the only democratic measure to increase between 2012 and 2021. The upward revision of the past alongside the downward revision of the future suggests a flattening of temporal perspective: the democratic trajectory, once perceived as steep and upward, now appeared level or gently declining. The temporal imagination that had characterized the 2012 moment — a past of darkness giving way to a present of transition and a future of democratic arrival — had been replaced by a more uniform assessment in which past, present, and near future occupied a narrow and unremarkable band.

Democracy satisfaction, however, rose to 3.10 — the highest value across all four waves, and a statistically significant increase from both the Wave 4 trough of 2.71 (p < 0.001) and the Wave 3 value of 2.98 (p < 0.001). On the surface, this is paradoxical: satisfaction with democracy reached its peak in the least democratic moment in the country's modern history, three years after a one-party election in which the CPP won every seat. The paradox dissolves under the normalization framework. Satisfaction is a function of the gap between expectations and perceived reality. In 2015, expectations remained elevated (democratic future: 7.72) while reality was visibly deteriorating, producing a large gap and low satisfaction. By 2021, expectations had collapsed (6.67) while the regime was delivering stability and modest service provision through its patronage infrastructure. The gap closed from below, and satisfaction rose. Citizens became more satisfied with "the way democracy works" not because democratic quality improved — by any external assessment it deteriorated dramatically — but because they had recalibrated their understanding of what "democracy" meant in the Cambodian context. The satisfaction recovery is the clearest single indicator of normalization in the dataset: it shows adaptive preference formation operating in real time, as citizens adjusted their evaluative standards to accommodate the available political reality.

## 8. NEW SECTION: Partisan Decomposition

### Partisan Heterogeneity and the Scope of Demobilization

If demobilization by subtraction operates specifically through the removal of the opposition — rather than through the generalized closure of political space — the resulting attitudinal shifts should concentrate among citizens who were politically attached to the dissolved party. Former CNRP supporters, who lost their vehicle for political expression, should exhibit steeper declines in democratic expectation and civic engagement than CPP supporters, whose political home remained intact. The ABS vote choice items, which identify respondents' reported partisan behavior in the most recent national election, permit a direct test of this prediction.

The partisan distribution across waves is itself the first piece of evidence. In Wave 4 (2015), CNRP voters constituted 21.3 percent of the sample and CPP voters 50.8 percent, with 17.2 percent nonvoters and 7.2 percent declining to answer. By Wave 6 (2021), the CNRP share had collapsed to 2.7 percent — 34 respondents, a residual so small it is analytically uninformative except as a marker of the dissolution's completeness. The CPP share returned to 60.1 percent, absorbing an unknown proportion of former CNRP supporters who reported voting CPP in the uncontested 2018 election. The DK/Refuse category nearly tripled, from 7.2 to 18.8 percent. The redistribution of the CNRP's former constituency — into CPP voting, nonvoting, or refusal to answer — *is* the subtraction documented at the level of individual survey responses.

The W4-to-W6 decomposition of key outcomes by partisan group yields a finding that initially appears to cut against the subtraction-specific mechanism: the shifts are broadly uniform. Contact with influential persons — the clearest measure of independent civic engagement — dropped by approximately 30 percentage points across all groups: 35.1 points among CPP voters, 30.9 among CNRP voters, 29.9 among nonvoters, and 25.6 among those declining to answer. Democratic future expectations declined by 1.27 to 1.39 points across groups. Single-party rule acceptance rose by 0.29 to 0.47 points. The absence of partisan heterogeneity is robust: no group was spared the demobilization, and no group experienced it with distinctive severity.

Two considerations complicate the interpretation of this uniformity as evidence against the subtraction mechanism. The first is methodological. The W6 partisan categories are themselves *products* of the dissolution. The 746 respondents classified as "CPP voters" in Wave 6 include an unknown number of former CNRP sympathizers who voted CPP in a one-party election — citizens for whom "CPP voter" is a post-dissolution category imposed by the absence of alternatives rather than a stable partisan identity. The DK/Refuse category, which tripled to 18.8 percent, likely contains the most resistant former CNRP supporters — those who could neither bring themselves to report voting CPP nor risk identifying with a criminalized party. Their democratic orientation profile in Wave 6 is consistent with this reading: they registered the lowest proportion selecting "democracy always preferable" (61.1 percent) and the highest rate of conditional authoritarianism (18.1 percent) of any group — a pattern more consistent with suppressed democratic preferences under duress than with genuine authoritarian conversion. The dissolution did not merely change attitudes; it destroyed the partisan categories through which differential effects could be measured.

The second consideration is theoretical, and it reframes the uniformity finding as consistent with — rather than contradictory to — the subtraction mechanism, once the mechanism is specified at the correct level of analysis. The prediction that CNRP supporters should be differentially affected assumes that subtraction operates like losing one's team: supporters of the dissolved party lose their political vehicle while everyone else is unaffected. The framework advanced in this article operates at a different level. The removal of the credible alternative restructures the political field itself — the horizon of possibility for all participants, not only those who identified with the opposition. CPP voters in 2015 inhabited a competitive political system in which their party could face genuine electoral challenge, in which political information served a decision-making function, and in which contacting influential persons outside the party apparatus had strategic rationale. CPP voters in 2021 inhabited a one-party state in which none of these conditions obtained. The elimination of the CNRP depoliticized the system as a whole, reducing the informational and participatory incentives for all citizens regardless of partisan attachment. Under this reading, the uniformity of the observed shifts is precisely what a field-level mechanism would predict — and what a party-level mechanism would not.

## 9. REVISED ALTERNATIVE MECHANISMS — Additions to Existing Subsections

### 9a. Addition to Preference Falsification Subsection

[Insert after the existing paragraph on the interaction between wave and perceived freedom of speech.]

The expanded democratic orientation battery provides additional leverage against the preference-falsification hypothesis. The response distributions on two forced-choice items are particularly informative. On the democracy-versus-equality trade-off, the Wave 6 shift moved respondents primarily into the noncommittal midpoint ("both equally important," expanding from 2.5 to 15.1 percent) rather than toward the pro-regime pole (which grew modestly). A frightened population seeking the socially safe answer in an authoritarian context would be expected to choose the answer that aligns with perceived regime preferences — in this case, prioritizing equality over political freedom, consistent with the CPP's developmental rhetoric. Instead, the largest category of movement was into ambivalence. Similarly, on the forced-choice democracy preference, the Wave 6 growth concentrated in "authoritarian sometimes preferable" (6.1 to 16.0 percent) rather than in "doesn't matter" (which actually continued to decline, from 18.9 to 17.5 percent). If survey reticence were the primary driver, the noncommittal option — the safest refuge for a respondent unwilling to stake a position — would be expected to expand. It contracted. The growth of conditional authoritarianism alongside the contraction of apathy is more consistent with citizens who have genuinely reassessed the trade-offs between regime types than with citizens concealing stable preferences behind safe responses.

### 9b. Addition to Nonresponse Discussion

[Insert after the existing discussion of elevated nonresponse on democratic expectations items. This partially replaces the current treatment of the 63% figure.]

The nonresponse gradient across the democratic orientation battery in Wave 6 is itself analytically informative. The items with the lowest nonresponse — "democracy is the best form of government" (9.9 percent), the democracy-versus-equality trade-off (8.1 percent), the forced-choice democracy preference (13.4 percent) — are agree/disagree or forced-choice items that require respondents to select among pre-defined positions. The items with the highest nonresponse — democratic future (63 percent), democratic present (42.5 percent) — are evaluative-projective items that require respondents to generate a numerical judgment about Cambodia's democratic trajectory. The cognitive and political demands of these two classes of items differ substantially. Placing Cambodia's democratic future on a ten-point scale requires both the political engagement to hold an opinion about the country's trajectory and the willingness to commit that opinion to a number. Selecting "agree" or "disagree" on whether democracy is the best form of government requires neither.

The nonresponse gradient thus maps onto the normalization framework. Citizens can still answer whether democracy is desirable in the abstract (low cost, low sensitivity), but increasingly decline to evaluate where their country sits on a democratic continuum (high cost, high sensitivity, or — per the theory — genuine absence of a formed opinion because the question has lost practical relevance). The 63 percent who did not answer the democratic future item and the 37 percent who answered with diminished expectations are not competing narratives but complementary expressions of the same process. The former have disengaged from the question entirely; the latter have revised their answer downward.

Bounds analysis confirms that the headline decline is robust to extreme assumptions about the missing respondents. ### 9c. New Subsection: Supplementary Evidence from the 2017 Socio-Economic Survey

[Insert as a new subsection within Alternative Mechanisms, or as a standalone bridging section.]

The 2017 Cambodia Socio-Economic Survey provides a second-instrument snapshot of civic orientations in the final months before the CNRP dissolution, partially addressing the temporal gap between ABS Waves 4 (2015) and 6 (2021). The SES data are not directly comparable to the ABS in scale or design, but the directional evidence is informative.

In early-to-mid 2017, 86.1 percent of SES respondents agreed that "people in this commune are free to speak what they think without fear," and 87.9 percent agreed that "people in this commune can join any organisation they like without fear." The perceived freedom to vote for any party without fear stood at 94.0 percent — near-universal agreement, recorded in the final months before the elimination of the only meaningful alternative party. These figures establish that on the eve of the crackdown, Cambodian citizens overwhelmingly perceived themselves as living in a civic environment that permitted free expression, association, and partisan choice. The political closure that followed was not acting on a population that already felt constrained. The subjective distance between the pre-dissolution civic environment (as perceived by citizens) and the post-dissolution environment documented in the ABS Wave 6 data was, by these measures, substantial.

The tea money item corroborates the corruption trajectory from a different instrument and a more behaviorally concrete measure. In the SES, 34.9 percent of respondents reported that they or a household member had paid additional money to government officials in 2016 to "get things done or get things done faster." This rate — measuring direct personal bribery experience rather than the broader "witnessed or experienced" formulation used in the ABS — confirms that petty corruption remained widespread in the immediate pre-dissolution period. The ABS witnessed-corruption item registered 62.8 percent in 2015 and 14.9 percent in 2021; the SES tea money rate of 34.9 percent for 2016, falling between these values on a narrower and more concrete measure, is consistent with a corruption environment that had not materially improved before the dissolution. The subsequent ABS collapse to 14.9 percent is thus more difficult to attribute to genuine anti-corruption progress and more consistent with the exposure-reduction and social-desirability mechanisms discussed above.

Satisfaction with local governance was high: 88.2 percent agreed that commune councillors are "helpful and responsive at solving problems," and 89.4 percent agreed that the village chief "generally works for the benefit of citizens." These figures are consistent with the CPP's patronage infrastructure functioning effectively at the commune level — the same infrastructure into which, the article argues, citizens redirected their engagement after the dissolution foreclosed independent channels. The post-dissolution rise in civil servant contact (from 11.0 percent in Wave 4 to 37.2 percent in Wave 6) landed on institutional ground that citizens already found functional.

The broader pattern of SES data availability reinforces the temporal argument. Detailed governance modules comparable to the items analyzed here were not publicly released for the 2018–2020 period; the only post-dissolution SES data available contain a single item on satisfaction with the government's handling of COVID-19. The systematic unavailability of civic orientation data from the years immediately following the CNRP dissolution — across both the ABS and the SES — reflects the same political closure the article documents and limits the temporal precision with which the attitudinal shifts can be dated.

## 10. REVISED CONCLUSION — Key Passages

[The first theoretical implication (mechanism) should be revised. The current text can be replaced with:]

The first concerns the mechanism itself, and the expanded democratic orientation analysis permits a more precise formulation than earlier treatments. Demobilization by subtraction does not operate by eroding democratic values. Abstract democratic commitment — measured by the most widely used items in the cross-national survey toolkit — remained essentially stable across the Cambodian study period; a clear majority of respondents in 2021 continued to endorse democracy as the best form of government at rates statistically indistinguishable from the peak mobilization of 2012. What collapsed was not the ideal but the perceived horizon within which the ideal could be realized, and the willingness to prioritize that ideal against competing goods. Democratic expectations fell by nearly three points on a ten-point scale. The pro-political-freedom share of the democracy-versus-equality trade-off contracted from 47 to 22 percent. And democracy satisfaction — paradoxically — rose to its highest recorded level, as citizens recalibrated their evaluative standards to accommodate a political reality in which the democratic alternative had been removed. The divergence between resilient ideals and collapsing prioritization suggests that democratic attitudes in authoritarian contexts are not the unitary construct that much of the democratic values literature assumes. They have at least three partially independent dimensions — ideal endorsement, practical prioritization, and feasibility expectation — that respond to the structure of available political alternatives in characteristically different ways. Cross-national surveys that aggregate these dimensions into a single "democratic support" index may be measuring a composite whose components move in opposite directions under precisely the conditions of greatest theoretical interest.

[The second implication (hope) should be revised:]

The second implication concerns the relationship between hope and democratic attitudes. The Wave 3 data, viewed alongside the expanded orientation battery, suggest that the extraordinary democratic optimism of 2012 was not accompanied by correspondingly extraordinary democratic commitment. The forced-choice democracy preference actually registered *lower* in 2012 than in 2015, when — counterintuitively — commitment surged to its highest level precisely as expectations were falling. This temporal decoupling suggests that democratic support in authoritarian contexts may be driven by two distinct psychological mechanisms that peak at different moments: feasibility perception, which responds to the structure of political opportunities and peaked when the CNRP made democracy appear imminent; and value crystallization, which responds to perceived threat and peaked when the post-2013 crackdown placed democratic values under visible siege. The Cambodian sequence — commitment lagging expectation on the upswing, then outlasting it on the downswing — challenges the foundational assumption of the political culture tradition that democratic values and democratic expectations move in tandem as expressions of a single underlying orientation.

## 13. THEORETICAL FRAMING ADDITIONS

### 13a. Elster on Adaptive Preferences

[Insert in the theoretical discussion, after the Gaventa/Lukes passage.]

The democracy-versus-equality trajectory — from near-parity in 2012 to a two-to-one margin favoring equality over political freedom by 2021 — bears the hallmarks of what Elster termed adaptive preference formation: the adjustment of desires to match perceived possibilities, the "sour grapes" mechanism through which agents come to devalue what they cannot obtain (Elster 1983). The Cambodian case, however, reveals a more complex process than Elster's formulation anticipates. The adaptation is partial. Citizens deprioritized political freedom when placed in explicit competition with equality, and they lowered their expectations of democratic progress, but they did not revise their abstract endorsement of democracy as the best form of government. The ideal persisted even as the practical commitment to it eroded. This partial adaptation is theoretically consequential: it suggests that democratic values operate at multiple psychological levels, some of which are more responsive to political context than others. The surface layer — abstract endorsement — proves resilient to even dramatic changes in the structure of political alternatives. The operational layer — the willingness to bear costs for democratic outcomes — proves highly elastic. A regime that successfully eliminates alternatives need not worry about what citizens believe in principle; it need only wait for the practical implications of that belief to atrophy.

### 13b. Expanded Wedeen Discussion

[Replace the current Wedeen paragraph with:]

The normalization framework finds a productive analogue in Wedeen's analysis of political compliance in Hafez al-Assad's Syria, where citizens participated in elaborate public rituals of regime praise not because they believed the regime's claims but because acting as if they believed was the condition of ordinary life (Wedeen 1998, 2000). The Cambodian case differs from Wedeen's Syria in a respect that is not incidental but analytically fundamental. Wedeen's compliance is performative: citizens maintain private skepticism while publicly enacting belief, producing a gap between inner conviction and outer behavior that defines the regime's mode of domination. The Cambodian pattern visible in the ABS data is not performative in this sense. The survey responses are private — administered in face-to-face interviews with confidentiality protocols, not in public settings where social surveillance operates. The attitudinal shifts documented here appear to reflect genuine reorientation, not strategic concealment: citizens moved into conditional authoritarianism rather than into noncommittal safe responses, and the shifts cut across demographic subgroups and partisan categories in ways that fear-based models do not predict. What the Cambodian case shares with Wedeen's Syria is not the mechanism but the outcome: a population that has arranged its cognitive and evaluative life around the available political reality, not because it has been persuaded of the regime's virtue but because it has incorporated the regime's permanence as a background condition of practical reasoning. The difference is that Wedeen's Syrians *performed* accommodation while potentially preserving private dissent; the Cambodian data suggest something closer to the internalization of accommodation — or at minimum, the erosion of the psychological infrastructure required to sustain dissent in the absence of institutional support.
