## Edit 1 — Introduction, "roadmap for the narrative structure" (p. 3)

This passage tells the reader what the narrative structure will do before doing it. The skill flags this as meta-narration and smooth expository scaffolding.

**OLD (L81–90):**
"The article proceeds as a structured analytic narrative, organized around the four survey waves as acts in a political drama. This is a deliberate methodological choice. Cambodia's trajectory between 2008 and 2021 has the unusual property of being both analytically tractable—the survey data capture discrete moments in a well-documented political sequence—and narratively coherent, with each wave mapping onto a distinct phase of political life. The narrative structure allows the article to preserve the temporal dynamics that conventional cross-sectional analysis tends to flatten, while the survey data anchor the story in systematic evidence rather than impressionistic accounts."

**NEW:**

Cambodia's trajectory between 2008 and 2021 is both analytically tractable — the survey data capture discrete moments in a well-documented closure — and narratively coherent, with each wave mapping onto a distinct phase of political life."

**Rationale:** Keeps the "deliberate methodological choice" declaration — which signals that the narrative structure is planned to put the political sequence into relief, not a stylistic indulgence — but cuts the two sentences that then over-explain why it's deliberate ("preserve the temporal dynamics that conventional cross-sectional analysis tends to flatten," "anchor the story in systematic evidence rather than impressionistic accounts"). The declaration is stronger standing alone. Saves ~40 words.

## Edit 2 — Introduction, "architectural vocabulary" paragraph (pp. 4–5)

**OLD (L91–103):**
"The question of how dominant-party regimes endure has generated a rich architectural vocabulary in contemporary scholarship. We are accustomed to analyzing the structural mechanics of survival: the skewed playing fields of competitive authoritarianism, the meticulous menu of manipulation required to manage electoral autocracies, and the stabilizing pillars of legitimation, repression, and co-optation. Yet, this institutional focus, while vital for understanding the resilience of the state, often obscures the quiet, downstream effects on the citizenry. By tracing the Cambodian electorate's trajectory from 2008 to 2021, it focuses not on how the regime maintained power, but on what happens to citizen orientations when the democratic alternative is systematically removed. Theories of regime survival treat the public as a variable to be managed — repressed into submission or bought off through patronage. This article asks a different question: what happens to citizen orientations when the democratic alternative is systematically removed? Removes "We are accustomed to analyzing the structural mechanics of survival" (meta-narration about the field's habits), "This article shifts the analytical lens" (meta-narration about what the article does), and the redundant "By tracing the Cambodian electorate's trajectory from 2008 to 2021, it focuses not on how the regime maintained power, but on" — which restates what the previous sentence already signaled. ## Edit 5 — W4 section, democratic orientation analysis (pp. 27–28)

This passage has a sustained logical chain — six consecutive sentences that each advance the argument incrementally with uniform pacing. The skill flags this (pattern #6).

**OLD (L949–962):**
"The democratic orientation measures from Wave 4 reveal the first signs of the decoupling that would define the Cambodian trajectory: the simultaneous intensification of abstract democratic commitment and erosion of democratic expectation. On the forced-choice democracy preference, 74.9 percent of respondents selected 'democracy is always preferable' — the highest proportion across all four waves, up sharply from 56.9 percent in 2012. The 'doesn't matter' category collapsed from 30.2 to 18.9 percent. Citizens who had been indifferent to the democracy question in earlier waves were now choosing sides, and choosing democracy. The shift is consistent with a political environment in which democracy had become contested and salient — the 2013 election, the mass protests, the crackdown — rather than the background condition it had been in 2008. Commitment to democracy as an ideal intensified precisely because the ideal was under visible threat. 'Democracy may have its problems but it is still the best form of government' dipped modestly from 3.26 to 3.15 (p < 0.001), a statistically significant but substantively small decline that left the mean firmly in 'agree' territory."

**NEW:**
"The democratic orientation measures from Wave 4 reveal the first signs of a decoupling that would define the Cambodian trajectory. On the forced-choice democracy preference, 74.9 percent of respondents selected 'democracy is always preferable' — the highest proportion across all four waves, up sharply from 56.9 percent in 2012. The 'doesn't matter' category collapsed from 30.2 to 18.9 percent. Citizens were choosing sides, and choosing democracy. Why? The 2013 election, the mass protests, the crackdown had made democracy contested and salient rather than the background condition it had been in 2008. Commitment intensified because the ideal was under visible threat. The second commitment item — 'democracy may have its problems but it is still the best form of government' — dipped modestly from 3.26 to 3.15 (p < 0.001), statistically significant but substantively small."

Cuts "a political environment in which" and "that left the mean firmly in 'agree' territory" (implied by the numbers). Saves ~40 words.

## Edit 6 — W6 authoritarian acceptance, "interpretive question" passage (pp. 38–39)

The "three considerations" structure is a detection risk (symmetric parallel structure, pattern #2). It also pre-announces its structure.

**OLD (L1590–1603):**
"There is an important interpretive question here, and the article's argument depends on how it is answered. Does the rise in authoritarian acceptance represent genuine preference change—Cambodians coming to believe that authoritarian governance is normatively desirable—or does it represent something else? Three considerations suggest the latter. First, the magnitude of the shift, while statistically meaningful, is modest in absolute terms: all four measures remain around 2.2 on a four-point scale, below the midpoint. Cambodians are not rating authoritarianism as 'good'; they are rating it as slightly less bad than before. Second, the timing—concentrated after the removal of the democratic alternative—suggests a mechanism closer to adaptive preference formation than genuine conversion. Third, and most importantly, the shift co-occurs with the collapse of democratic future expectations (from 7.72 to 6.67), rising perceptions of government information withholding, and declining political interest. This is not the attitudinal profile of a population that has found a new political faith; it is the profile of a population that has stopped believing the old one was achievable."

**NEW:**
"Does the rise in authoritarian acceptance represent genuine preference change — Cambodians coming to believe that authoritarian governance is normatively desirable — or something else? The magnitude of the shift, while statistically meaningful, is modest in absolute terms: all four measures remain around 2.2 on a four-point scale, below the midpoint. Cambodians are not rating authoritarianism as 'good'; they are rating it as slightly less bad than before. The timing matters too — concentrated after the removal of the democratic alternative, not during an earlier period of repressive tightening — pointing toward adaptive preference formation rather than genuine conversion. Most telling is the attitudinal context: the shift co-occurs with the collapse of democratic future expectations (from 7.72 to 6.67) and declining political interest. This is not a population that has found a new political faith. It is a population that has stopped believing the old one was achievable."

**Rationale:** Cuts the meta-narration opening ("There is an important interpretive question here, and the article's argument depends on how it is answered") — the question itself does that work. Eliminates "Three considerations suggest the latter" and the First/Second/Third enumeration, instead flowing the evidence as prose with varied connective tissue ("The magnitude...," "The timing matters too...," "Most telling is..."). Final two sentences broken apart for emphasis. Saves ~45 words.

**OLD (L2209–2225):**

Yet this limitation is also, in a sense, the study's most striking finding. The silence of the 63 percent and the diminished expectations of the 37 percent who answered are not competing narratives but complementary expressions of the same process. Both constitute evidence of a citizenry educated out of democratic expectation, albeit through different registers of withdrawal."

**NEW:**
"A further caution concerns the 63 percent nonresponse on the democratic future item in Wave 6: the reported 2021 mean is effectively a survivor statistic. Yet this limitation doubles as a finding. If demobilization by subtraction narrows the horizon of political possibility, then the inability of nearly two-thirds of respondents to venture an opinion about Cambodia's democratic future is not merely a data problem — it is the condition the framework predicts. The silence of the 63 percent and the diminished expectations of the 37 percent who answered are complementary expressions of the same process."

## Edit 9 — Conclusion, comparative extension paragraph (pp. 64–65)

**OLD (L2232–2249):**
"The most productive response to this limitation is comparative extension. The demobilization sequence observed in Cambodia—declining democratic expectations, followed by collapsing independent civic engagement, followed by rising authoritarian acceptance—generates a testable temporal ordering that can be examined in other cases of opposition elimination. Turkey after the suppression of the Peoples' Democratic Party (HDP), Russia following the dismantling of Navalny's organizational network, and Thailand in the years following the 2014 coup all represent contexts where credible political alternatives were systematically removed from the political field. The Asian Barometer dataset itself offers the most direct comparative leverage: Thailand's post-coup trajectory and the Philippines under Duterte provide cases where similar dynamics may be observable using identical survey instruments. The turnout-participation divergence identified here—rising electoral compliance amid collapsing civic engagement—offers a particularly tractable diagnostic, as it requires only two commonly available survey measures and generates a clear empirical prediction. Future research should test whether the Cambodian sequence represents a general pattern of authoritarian normalization or a configuration specific to the conditions of this particular case."

**NEW:**
"The most productive response is comparative extension. The Cambodian sequence — declining expectations, collapsing civic engagement, rising authoritarian acceptance — generates a testable temporal ordering applicable wherever credible alternatives have been eliminated: Turkey after the HDP suppression, Russia after the dismantling of Navalny's network, Thailand after the 2014 coup. The Asian Barometer dataset offers direct comparative leverage, with Thailand's post-coup trajectory and the Philippines under Duterte providing cases where identical instruments capture the relevant dynamics. The turnout-participation divergence — rising electoral compliance amid collapsing civic engagement — is a particularly tractable diagnostic, requiring only two commonly available survey measures."

**Rationale:** The original says "this limitation" (unclear referent at paragraph start), lists three cases and then lists two of them again from a different angle. The revision consolidates the case list and removes the closing sentence ("Future research should test whether..."), which is generic and adds nothing the reader hasn't already inferred. Saves ~70 words.
