## A. Causal Language: Dissolution as Keystone vs. Standalone Cause

The reviewer's core concern (Critique #2): the paper sometimes slides between "the dissolution caused this" and "the broader closure process caused this." The research design cannot distinguish the dissolution as a standalone shock from the dissolution as the keystone event in a broader repression sequence that also included media shutdowns, LANGO enforcement, civil society restrictions, and the 2018 sham election. The revisions below sharpen the framing without retreating from the theoretical contribution. The guiding principle: be precise about what the dissolution *was* (the act that restructured the political field) and what the data *can show* (that the timing, direction, and domain-specificity of shifts are consistent with subtraction and inconsistent with alternatives) without overclaiming that the dissolution alone, as a single event, produced all observed changes.

---

### Revision A1 — Abstract (p. 1)

The abstract currently attributes the outcome directly to "the removal of credible democratic alternatives." This should acknowledge the broader closure process.

**OLD (p. 1):**
"The Cambodian case offers an unusually clean empirical window into how dominant-party authoritarian regimes produce political quiescence — not through the cultivation of enthusiastic support, but through a process the article terms demobilization by subtraction, in which the removal of credible democratic alternatives restructures citizen orientations across multiple attitudinal and behavioral dimensions simultaneously."

**NEW:**
"The Cambodian case offers an unusually clean empirical window into how dominant-party authoritarian regimes produce political quiescence — not through the cultivation of enthusiastic support, but through a process the article terms demobilization by subtraction. The forced dissolution of the Cambodia National Rescue Party in 2017 was the keystone of a broader campaign of political closure — encompassing independent media shutdowns, civil society restrictions, and an uncontested national election — that collectively restructured citizen orientations across multiple attitudinal and behavioral dimensions simultaneously."

**Rationale:** Names the dissolution as the anchoring event while situating it within the wider closure sequence. More precise, and pre-empts the reviewer's objection without conceding the theoretical point.

### Revision A2 — Introduction, core claim (p. 2)

**OLD (p. 2):**
"The answer, stated plainly, is that the Cambodian People's Party dissolved the only credible opposition party, the Cambodia National Rescue Party (CNRP), in November 2017, and in doing so restructured the political field so thoroughly that Cambodian citizens adjusted their attitudes, behaviors, and expectations in a remarkably coherent pattern."

**NEW:**
"The answer, stated plainly, is that the Cambodian People's Party dissolved the only credible opposition party, the Cambodia National Rescue Party (CNRP), in November 2017 — and simultaneously dismantled the independent media, civil society organizations, and informal networks through which citizens had accessed alternative political information and organized collective action. The combined effect restructured the political field so thoroughly that Cambodian citizens adjusted their attitudes, behaviors, and expectations in a remarkably coherent pattern."

**Rationale:** The original formulation treats the dissolution as a standalone sufficient cause. The revision treats it as the centerpiece of a broader closure campaign, which is both empirically more accurate and consistent with the paper's own account on pp. 30–31. The theoretical claim (demobilization by subtraction) is preserved — the mechanism is still the removal of the alternative, not repression or co-optation — but the vehicle is specified as the full closure sequence rather than the single judicial act.

### Revision A3 — Introduction, observable signature paragraph (p. 3)

**OLD (p. 3):**
"This article proposes that opposition elimination produces a distinctive observable signature in public opinion: synchronized, multi-domain shifts in which democratic expectations, independent civic engagement, political attention, and willingness to report corruption all decline in parallel, while acceptance of authoritarian governance alternatives and participation through regime-managed channels rise."

**NEW:**

**Rationale:** The parenthetical clarification anchors "opposition elimination" as a process rather than an event, which is what the Cambodian data actually show (shifts beginning W3→W4, accelerating W4→W6).

### Revision A4 — Demobilization by subtraction definition (p. 5)

This is the theoretical core. The current definition is clean and should be preserved, but a sentence should be added to clarify scope.

**OLD (p. 5):**
"Its proximate cause is not an action taken against citizens but an action taken against the political field itself: the removal of the credible alternative around which civic mobilization had organized."

**NEW (insert after the existing sentence):**
"Its proximate cause is not an action taken against citizens but an action taken against the political field itself: the removal of the credible alternative around which civic mobilization had organized. In practice, subtraction rarely occurs as a single surgical act; the Cambodian case involved the coordinated elimination of the opposition party, independent media outlets, and civil society infrastructure within a compressed period. The theoretical claim is that the decisive mechanism is the closure of the political field — the elimination of the vehicle for collective political expression — rather than the fear, material deprivation, or ideological conversion that characterize repression and co-optation respectively."

**Rationale:** This addition does three things. It acknowledges that the dissolution was part of a broader campaign (satisfying the reviewer). It preserves the theoretical distinction from repression and co-optation (protecting the contribution). And it specifies the level at which the mechanism operates (field closure, not any single component), which also sets up the partisan decomposition argument later.

### Revision A5 — Alternative mechanisms section, new subsection (pp. 46–59)

The reviewer wants an explicit discussion of the competing temporal models the data cannot adjudicate between. This is best handled as a short addition to the alternative mechanisms section, after the pandemic discussion and before the technological channel substitution discussion. Proposed insertion after p. 57, paragraph 1:

**NEW SUBSECTION — "Temporal resolution and the dissolution-as-keystone interpretation":**

"A distinct methodological concern cuts across all of the preceding adjudication tests: the six-year gap between Wave 4 (2015) and Wave 6 (2021) means the analysis cannot determine the temporal profile of the observed shifts. Three models are consistent with the available data. The first — an acute shock model — holds that the sharpest attitudinal changes occurred in the immediate aftermath of the CNRP dissolution in November 2017 and the one-party election of July 2018, and that the 2021 data capture a population that has already partially recovered from the initial shock. Under this model, the 2021 values understate the peak magnitude of demobilization. The second — a gradual normalization model — holds that the shifts accumulated incrementally across the 2017–2021 period as the consequences of political closure compounded through informational feedback loops and the progressive atrophy of civic habits. Under this model, the 2021 data capture the endpoint of a process still in motion. The third — a repression-with-subtraction model — holds that the observed shifts are not primarily products of field restructuring but of the cumulative repressive apparatus that accompanied the dissolution: media closures, civil society restrictions, legal harassment, and the chilling effects of the criminalization of the opposition. Under this model, subtraction is a component of a standard authoritarian consolidation package rather than a distinctive mechanism.

The available data cannot definitively adjudicate among these three models, and the honest answer is that elements of all three likely operated simultaneously. Two considerations, however, favor the subtraction-centered interpretation over the repression-centered alternative. First, the attitudinal trajectory began before the most intensive period of repression: democratic future expectations fell by nearly two points and political interest declined measurably between Wave 3 (2012) and Wave 4 (2015), a period during which the CNRP was under selective pressure but still functionally intact and the broader repressive apparatus had not yet been deployed. If cumulative repression were the primary driver, the onset of attitudinal change should concentrate in the post-2017 period rather than emerging incrementally from the peak of mobilization. Second, the domain-specificity of the shifts — political attitudes and behaviors declining while non-political attitudes remain stable — is more consistent with a mechanism that operates through the structure of political alternatives than with generalized intimidation, which would be expected to depress expressed attitudes across domains. The interpretation advanced here treats the dissolution as the keystone of the closure process: the act that restructured the political field and set in motion the cascading adjustments documented in the data, even as the accompanying repressive measures amplified and accelerated those adjustments."

**Rationale:** This is the most important single addition to the paper. It gives the reviewer exactly what was asked for — an explicit treatment of competing temporal models — while landing on the subtraction interpretation through evidence rather than assertion. The key analytical moves are: (a) naming three distinct temporal models rather than treating the concern as a vague limitation; (b) acknowledging that the data cannot fully distinguish them; (c) identifying two empirical considerations that favor the subtraction-centered reading. The "keystone" framing threads the needle: it credits the dissolution as the decisive event without claiming it was the sole cause, and it distinguishes the mechanism (field restructuring) from the accompanying repressive apparatus.

The Cambodian closure involved the simultaneous elimination of the opposition party, independent media, and civil society organizations — a compressed sequence whose individual contributions cannot be isolated with the available data. **Rationale:** This places the scope clarification in the conclusion where it will be most visible to the reviewer, and where it belongs theoretically — as a statement about the mechanism's scope conditions rather than as a limitation.

## B. W6 Expectations Demotion

The reviewer's concern (Critique #4): the methods section correctly designates the expectations items as "supporting evidence for patterns that are established more robustly on the commitment, prioritization, and satisfaction measures" (p. 12), but the W6 narrative section (pp. 35–38) doesn't consistently follow this hierarchy. The expectations data carry 42–63% nonresponse in W6 and should not bear primary argumentative weight. The revisions below demote expectations to supporting/illustrative status and elevate the satisfaction paradox and prioritization collapse as the primary evidence.

---

### Revision B1 — Section 1.1.7 opening (p. 35)

The section currently opens by noting that commitment survived the dissolution, then moves to the forced-choice item, then to prioritization, then to expectations. This ordering is correct. But the transition to expectations needs a stronger caveat.

**OLD (p. 37):**
"The democratic expectation items continued their descent, though the estimates must be interpreted with caution given the elevated nonresponse rates discussed below."

**NEW:**
"The democratic expectation items continued their descent, though these estimates rest on substantially weaker data foundations than the commitment, prioritization, and satisfaction measures. With 63 percent nonresponse on the democratic future item and 43 percent on the democratic present item in Wave 6, the expectation measures describe the orientation of the minority of respondents who retained enough political engagement to formulate a numerical judgment about Cambodia's democratic trajectory. The patterns are reported here as supporting evidence for the normalization dynamics established above on more robust measures, not as independent findings."

**Rationale:** This is the most important demotion. The original caveat ("must be interpreted with caution") is too weak given 63% missingness. The revision makes the data hierarchy explicit at the point of first use in the W6 section, rather than relying on the reader to remember the methods section.

---

### Revision B2 — "Flattening of temporal perspective" passage (pp. 37–38)

This is the passage the reviewer specifically flagged as overinterpreting a selected subsample.

**OLD (pp. 37–38):**
"The upward revision of the past alongside the downward revision of the future suggests a flattening of temporal perspective: the democratic trajectory, once perceived as steep and upward, now appeared level or gently declining."

**NEW:**
"Among respondents who answered, the upward revision of the past alongside the downward revision of the future is suggestive of a flattened temporal perspective — though this interpretation necessarily rests on a selected subsample and should be treated as illustrative rather than definitive."

**Rationale:** Hedges the interpretation appropriately without deleting it. The pattern is genuinely interesting and worth reporting; it just shouldn't be stated as a finding given the selection problem.

---

### Revision B3 — Satisfaction paradox elevation (p. 38)

The satisfaction paradox is currently given roughly equal space as the expectations decline. It should be marked as the primary W6 finding, since satisfaction carries only 6.4% nonresponse.

**OLD (p. 38):**
"Democracy satisfaction, however, rose to 3.10 — the highest value across all four waves, and a statistically significant increase from both the Wave 4 trough of 2.71 (p < 0.001) and the Wave 3 value of 2.98 (p < 0.001)."

**NEW:**
"The most robust single finding from the Wave 6 democratic orientation battery — measured on an item with only 6.4 percent nonresponse, on identical wording across all four waves — is the satisfaction paradox. Democracy satisfaction rose to 3.10, the highest value across all four waves and a statistically significant increase from both the Wave 4 trough of 2.71 (p < 0.001) and the Wave 3 value of 2.98 (p < 0.001)."

**Rationale:** Explicitly flags this as the headline finding on data-quality grounds. The parenthetical about nonresponse and identical wording gives the reader the information needed to assess relative evidentiary weight.

---

### Revision B4 — Prioritization collapse elevation (p. 37)

Similarly, the prioritization collapse (8.1% nonresponse) should be marked as carrying near-primary evidentiary weight.

**OLD (p. 37):**
"Democratic prioritization reached its lowest recorded level."

**NEW:**
"Democratic prioritization — measured on an item with 8.1 percent nonresponse, among the lowest rates in the political battery — reached its lowest recorded level."

**Rationale:** Brief parenthetical that signals data quality without disrupting the narrative flow.

---

### Revision B5 — Section 1.1.11 (Conclusion), first implication (p. 60)

The conclusion's first theoretical implication currently gives expectations and prioritization roughly equal billing. It should lead with the measures that have the strongest data foundations.

**OLD (p. 60):**
"What collapsed was not the ideal but the perceived horizon within which the ideal could be realized, and the willingness to prioritize that ideal against competing goods. Democratic expectations fell by nearly three points on a ten-point scale. The pro-political-freedom share of the democracy-versus-equality trade-off contracted from 47 to 22 percent. And democracy satisfaction — paradoxically — rose to its highest recorded level..."

**NEW:**
"What collapsed was not the ideal but the willingness to prioritize that ideal against competing goods — and, on the more tentatively measured expectation items, the perceived horizon within which the ideal could be realized. The pro-political-freedom share of the democracy-versus-equality trade-off contracted from 47 to 22 percent. Democracy satisfaction — paradoxically — rose to its highest recorded level, as citizens recalibrated their evaluative standards to accommodate a political reality in which the democratic alternative had been removed. Democratic expectations, measured on items carrying substantially higher nonresponse, fell by nearly three points on a ten-point scale — a decline that, while directionally robust even under extreme imputation assumptions, rests on a selected subsample and corroborates rather than independently establishes the normalization pattern."

**Rationale:** Reorders the evidence hierarchy: prioritization first (8.1% nonresponse), satisfaction second (6.4%), expectations third with an explicit data-quality caveat. This is the ordering the methods section promised; the conclusion should deliver on it.
